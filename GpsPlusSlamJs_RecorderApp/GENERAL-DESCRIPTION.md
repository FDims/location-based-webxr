### DESCRIPTION
 Adding a Measurement Points feature to the recorder app (GpsPlusSlamJs_RecorderApp): a way for the user to mark a point in 3D space that they cannot physically touch — a spot up high, across the street, or hundreds of meters away — by shooting rays at it from different positions and letting the app triangulate where those rays best meet, fused with the phone's depth sensing. It is the natural next step beyond the existing reference-point flow, which can only mark places you can walk the phone onto.

### USE CASES
- **Short Range(depth dominated)**: The target is a few meters away, the depth map is reliable, and even a single well-aimed ray plus its depth reading already pins the point down well. More rays refine it.
- **Long range (parallax-dominated)**: The target is 100–200 m away, where depth is useless. The user marks it, walks several meters to the side (say ~10 m), and marks it again — and again. With a wide enough baseline between those vantage points, the rays triangulate the far point even though no single measurement could.

### GOALS
- While recording, the user points the phone at something they want to mark — a fixed crosshair in the middle of the screen, or a tap on the specific target in the camera feed — and "shoots" a ray. The app captures the real AR device pose at that instant (the same pose stream the recorder already reads every frame) and turns it into a ray into the world, drawn as a line, and at the same moment samples the depth map along that exact aimed direction to get a rough distance to the target. From the very first ray it already shows a provisional measurement sphere — seeded by that depth reading — with a live uncertainty / convergence readout next to it. As the user moves and shoots more rays from different positions, the app re-solves the point as the closest point of approach of all the rays, with the depth readings folded in as a distance-weighted prior (trusted up close, fading out with range, and progressively out-voted by the triangulation as the baseline grows), and with robust outlier rejection discarding bad rays. The sphere tightens and the uncertainty shrinks; for far targets the app actively coaches the user ("move sideways and mark again") until the baseline is wide enough. When happy, the user confirms the point. It is stored as a new Measurement Point — reusing the recorder's per-scenario point storage and its GPS-world sphere visualization — and rendered as two connected dots: one in the AR-local frame and one in the GPS-world frame, the small gap between them doubling as a live alignment-quality probe. Its primary purpose is to be a handy in-the-field measuring/marking tool for points you cannot reach; the cross-session persistence and the alignment diagnostic come along essentially for free.

### ARCHITECTURE
- The estimator interface (the pure heart, lifted from Phase 1). A pure function from a set of observations — each ray (origin, direction) plus an optional depth-derived point and a weight — to a result (point, plus an uncertainty / error metric), all in one consistent coordinate frame and with no Three.js, no DOM, no WebXR. This is the same closest-point-of-approach solver you wrote in Phase 1, generalized to carry weights and a depth prior. Everything downstream is tested against this interface, and it is the single most reusable artifact you produce (a candidate upstream PR — it is GPS-free, framework-free math).

- The MeasurementPoint entity (what gets persisted). Model it on the recorder's existing RefPointDefinition / RefPointObservation so you can reuse the storage and visualization rather than inventing new ones. A measurement point records the rays that produced it (each with the device pose and the depth observation used), the solved AR-local position, the derived GPS-world position, the final uncertainty, and the usual ids/timestamps/session links. Decide early whether these live in the same per-scenario refPoints/ directory (with a type discriminator) or a sibling measurementPoints/ directory — a small schema decision to bring to a review call.

- The Redux action model (reuse the recorder's library store, don't fork it). Mirror the Phase 1 shape on top of the recorder's existing gps-plus-slam-js store: an addMeasurementRay action (capture the pose + depth observation), a deliberate compute/refine step, confirmMeasurementPoint, deleteMeasurementPoint, and undo/redo — and, like every other recorder action, persisted to disk so the whole marking flow replays deterministically. Do not create a custom store or record rays outside the library store; that breaks replay (see the recorder README's "Common Mistakes").

### COMPONENTS
1. Ray-triangulation core (pure — ported from your Phase 1 app). The closest-point-of-approach solver: given N weighted rays in one frame, compute the best-fit point and a meaningful uncertainty / convergence metric (reuse and refine the error metric you designed in Phase 1). Generalize it to accept a depth-derived point + weight alongside the rays so the later fusion (component 3) has a single clean entry point. No Three.js, no DOM.
    1.1. Test: unit-test against known geometry — two exactly intersecting rays return the intersection with ~zero error; near-parallel rays return a sensible point with high uncertainty; adding a third consistent ray lowers the uncertainty; weights bias the result as expected. (This is essentially your Phase 1 test suite, extended.)
    1.2. Demo: a plain scene (no AR) where you place a few rays by hand and see the computed point + error — the Phase 1 app itself, repurposed as the demo harness.
2. Depth-prior provider (on-demand depth at the aimed pixel). At shoot-time, sample the recorder's existing depth map / occupancy grid along the aimed direction (the crosshair pixel, or the tapped pixel), unproject it to a 3D point on the ray, and attach a confidence weight that decays with distance (§2.5.3). Reuse the recorder's depth-sampling rather than adding a parallel one.
    2.1. Test: unit-test the pure unprojection (a known depth value + pixel + camera intrinsics → the expected 3D point on the ray) and the confidence/weight model (weight high for near depth, decaying to ~0 at long range; "no usable depth" handled cleanly). The live depth read is view-layer.
    2.2. Demo: aim at a near object and a far one and print the depth point + its confidence; show the confidence collapsing at distance.
3. Depth-weighted fusion + robust estimation (the genuinely new heart). Combine the rays (component 1) and the per-ray depth points (component 2) into one estimate: depth as a distance-weighted soft prior — strong up close, fading with range, and progressively out-voted by triangulation as the baseline grows — with mandated robust outlier rejection (RANSAC is the reference approach; the exact method, sample model and thresholds are yours to choose and justify, §2.5.4). The single-ray-plus-depth case (a candidate first iteration) should fall out of this as the degenerate case.
    3.1. Test: unit-test on synthetic scenes with a known answer: a clean multi-ray set converges to it; an injected outlier ray is rejected and does not move the result; with one ray the depth prior dominates, and as good rays accumulate the solution moves off the depth prior toward the triangulated point. Determinism where the method allows (seed any sampling).
    3.2. Demo: scrub from 1 → N rays (with a deliberately bad one mixed in) and watch the estimate and uncertainty evolve, the outlier get rejected, and depth's influence fade.
4. Aiming + ray capture in AR (crosshair + tap). Turn a "shoot" into a ray: capture the device pose at that instant (reuse the recorder's getCurrentArPose()), and build the ray either along the device-forward direction (a fixed center crosshair — point the whole phone) or through a tapped pixel (unproject via the camera intrinsics). Support both, crosshair as the steady default and tap for picking a specific target without re-aiming the phone. Draw each ray as a line in the scene.
    4.1. Test: unit-test the pure ray-construction math, especially tap unprojection (a tapped pixel + the WebXR projection → the correct world-space ray) and the crosshair = camera-forward case, against known inputs. The live pose read and the line rendering are view-layer.
    4.2. Demo: in AR (or by replaying a walk on desktop), shoot a few rays both ways and see the lines appear from the correct poses.
5. Live measurement UX + baseline coaching. The interaction loop: a provisional sphere appears from the first ray+depth and re-solves and tightens as rays accumulate (the Phase 1 feedback loop, now real-time), with a live uncertainty readout; for far targets, actively coach the user to build parallax ("move sideways and mark again") and show the uncertainty shrinking as the baseline grows; an explicit Confirm commits the point. Keep the decision logic pure and testable; the on-screen widgets are view-layer.
    5.1. Test: unit-test the pure state/decision logic — when to show "needs more baseline" vs. "good enough", how the uncertainty maps to the coaching prompt, the provisional→confirmed transition. Feed it synthetic sequences of observations.
    5.2. Demo: replay a walk that marks a far point from several spots and watch the prompt, the shrinking uncertainty, and the tightening sphere — no phone needed.
6. Measurement-point entity: persistence + dual-frame visualization. Persist a confirmed point as the §2.2 MeasurementPoint, reusing the recorder's per-scenario point storage and sphere-visualization infra. Solve in the AR-local frame, then store both the AR-local position and the GPS-world position (via the alignment matrix), and render them as two connected dots: the gap between them is a live alignment-error probe (the same spirit as the recorder's existing raw-GPS-vs-fused marker pair). Wire delete + undo/redo.
    6.1. Test: a round-trip test (confirm a point → persist → reload → recover an equivalent entity, with both frames intact); the AR↔GPS conversion is correct against a known alignment matrix; undo/redo restores prior state.
    6.2. Demo: mark and confirm a point, reload, and see it come back as the two connected dots; show the gap reacting as the alignment matrix updates.
7. Recorder integration + replay-based validation. Wire 1–6 into the recorder's record/replay loop: the new actions persist to disk like every other recorder action, so a recorded session (which already logs depth) deterministically replays the entire marking flow on desktop. This is what proves the feature is plugged in correctly, not just that a live demo happened to work once.
    7.1. Test: an e2e replay test — replay a Task 1 recording that marks a point, and assert the same Measurement Point (position within tolerance, uncertainty) is reproduced deterministically. The unit-level math is already covered by 1–6.
    7.2. Demo: the feature running inside the recorder on a phone outdoors and the same session replayed on desktop producing the identical point.
